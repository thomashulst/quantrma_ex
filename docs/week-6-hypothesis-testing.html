6 Week 6: Hypothesis Testing

You’ve made it to the final lab of this course! This week is all about hypothesis testing, one of the most ubiquitous elements of statistical theory, and the conclusion to your journey into inferential statistics (for now…). Almost any quantitative research paper you will read will feature a hypothesis test in one form or another. Furthermore, the quantitative research you might conduct in other courses and/or your capstone will rely heavily on the framework hypothesis testing. During this lab we will first discuss the theories at the foundation of hypothesis testing. In the second part of the lab we are going to practice with conducting null hypothesis significance tests (NHSTs) in R.

To get started, download the lab template here (right click: save as) or from Canvas. Copy the lab template to your lab folder and double click the Lab project file (not the .Rmd) to open RStudio.

6.1 Learning goals

During this lab you will do the following:

  1. Discuss various concepts related to hypothesis testing
  2. Conduct one-sample, paired-samples and independent samples \(t\)-tests in R

6.3 \(t\)-tests in R

I’ll be honest. Conducting a \(t\)-test in R can be slightly painful. Part of that has to do with the fact that the t.test() function was not really written with “tidy data” in mind (which we have become used to over the past couple of weeks), but also because it can do more than just one thing. It can actually do three different types of \(t\)-test (more even, but we focus on the three here) so the function is quite specific in how it expects its arguments depending on which test you want it to perform. How to use t.test() is actually explained quite extensively in the textbook, so I will not go over all the details here, but I will highlight some useful tips while conducting \(t\)-tests in R.

Remember the three types of \(t\)-test we can conduct are: the one-sample \(t\)-test, the paired samples \(t\)-test and the independent samples \(t\)-test. The one-sample \(t\)-test is simplest to use in R. Suppose we want to compare the mean grade of one group of students to a hypothesized mean. In that case, t.test() expects two arguments: x (a vector with your data) and mu (the value of the null hypothesis).

set.seed(123)
grades <- rnorm(n = 10, 75, 5) # generate 10 grades
null <- 60 # value of null hypothesis

results <- t.test(x = grades, mu = null)

print(results)
## 
##  One Sample t-test
## 
## data:  grades
## t = 10.194, df = 9, p-value = 3.049e-06
## alternative hypothesis: true mean is not equal to 60
## 95 percent confidence interval:
##  71.96165 78.78461
## sample estimates:
## mean of x 
##  75.37313

We have saved the results of the \(t\)-test in a variable called results. If we want to work with a specific result (for example the confidence interval), we can do that as follows:

results$conf.int
## [1] 71.96165 78.78461
## attr(,"conf.level")
## [1] 0.95
